2. ENHANCED EXAMPLE (The "Survey" Analogy):
-------------------------------------------
Imagine you want to find the average height of students in a school, class-wise (e.g., 2020, 2021).

1. THE MAPPER (The Data Collectors): You hire 10 people. They go to classrooms. For every student, they write "Year 2020: 160cm" on a small slip of paper.
2. THE SORT (The Organizer): You take all those thousands of slips and put them in a line. Because you "sort" them, all "2020" slips appear first, then all "2021" slips.
3. THE REDUCER (The Calculator): You give the stack of "2020" slips to a person with a calculator. They add up the heights and divide by the number of slips to get the average. Then they find which stack was the tallest.

3. SIMPLIFIED CONCEPT EXPLANATION:
----------------------------------
Hadoop-style MapReduce is used to process huge amounts of data (Big Data) that are too big for one computer's memory. 

In this weather practical:
- We have a CSV file with years and temperatures.
- **MAPPER**: Extracts the "Year" and "Temperature" from every row.
- **SORT**: Groups all temperatures from the same year together.
- **REDUCER**: Calculates the average temperature for each year and finds the Coolest and Hottest years overall.

4. DETAILED CODE SNIPPET EXPLANATION (For Examiner):
----------------------------------------------------

--- MAPPER (mapper.py) ---

[CODE]
for line in sys.stdin:
    line = line.strip()
    if line.startswith("year"):
        continue
[EXPLANATION]
1. `sys.stdin`: Reads the data line-by-line as it is piped in from the CSV file.
2. `startswith("year")`: This is very important. It skips the very first line of the CSV (the header) so we don't try to calculate the temperature of the word "temperature".

[CODE]
parts = line.split(",")
year = parts[0].strip()
temp = float(parts[1].strip())
print(f"{year}\t{temp}")
[EXPLANATION]
1. `split(",")`: Breaks each comma-separated line into two pieces: Year and Temperature.
2. `float(temp)`: Converts the temperature string (like "30.5") into a real number.
3. `print(...)`: Outputs the "Key" (Year) and "Value" (Temperature) separated by a Tab. This is the standard MapReduce output format.

--- REDUCER (reducer.py) ---

[CODE]
for line in sys.stdin:
    year, temp = line.split("\t")
    if year not in year_temps:
        year_temps[year] = []
    year_temps[year].append(float(temp))
[EXPLANATION]
1. `split("\t")`: Reads the incoming lines from the sorted mapper output.
2. `year_temps`: A dictionary where each Key is a Year, and the Value is a **LIST** of all temperatures seen for that year. We append every temperature to the list.

[CODE]
for year, temps in year_temps.items():
    avg_temps[year] = sum(temps) / len(temps)
[EXPLANATION]
Once all data is read, we iterate through our dictionary and calculate the average for each year (Total Temperature / Number of Records).

[CODE]
coolest_year = min(avg_temps, key=avg_temps.get)
hottest_year = max(avg_temps, key=avg_temps.get)
[EXPLANATION]
These functions look at all the averages we just calculated and find the year that has the lowest (min) and highest (max) average temperature.

5. VIVA QUESTIONS AND ANSWERS:
------------------------------
Q1: Why is MapReduce suitable for weather data?
A: Weather data is massive (collected every minute for decades). MapReduce can process this data in parallel across multiple servers, making it much faster.

Q2: What is the key and value in the mapper output?
A: The **Key** is the 'Year' and the **Value** is the 'Temperature'.

Q3: Why do we use `try-except` in the mapper/reducer?
A: Real-world data is often "dirty" (missing values or typos). The `try-except` ensures that if one line is broken, the program just skips it instead of crashing.

Q4: What is the benefit of the "Sort" phase?
A: It ensures that all data for the same year arrives at the Reducer together. This allows the Reducer to process one year completely before moving to the next.

Q5: Can we find the Maximum temperature of all time using this?
A: Yes. We would simply change the reducer logic to use `max()` on the list of all temperatures instead of calculating the average.
